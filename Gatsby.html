F. Scott Fitzgerald

September 24, 1896 - December 21, 1940

Nationality: American

Notable Works: Besides "The Great Gatsby," Fitzgerald is known for novels such as "This Side of Paradise," "Tender Is the Night," and short stories like "The Curious Case of Benjamin Button." He is often associated with the "Roaring Twenties" and the Jazz Age.

Literary Style: Fitzgerald's writing is characterized by its exploration of the American Dream, decadence, and the societal changes of the early 20th century. His work often delves into the complexities of wealth, love, and the pursuit of happiness.

The Great Gatsby

Published: 1949

Summary of "The Great Gatsby":

"The Great Gatsby" is a classic American novel set in the summer of 1922, narrated by Nick Carraway. The story primarily revolves around the mysterious Jay Gatsby and his unrequited love for Daisy Buchanan. Here's a detailed summary:

  1. Introduction of Nick Carraway:
    The novel begins with Nick Carraway, the narrator, moving to West Egg, Long Island, in the summer of 1922. He rents a small house next to Jay Gatsby's mansion and becomes friends with his enigmatic neighbor.
  2. The Buchanans and Tom's Affair:
    Nick reconnects with his cousin, Daisy Buchanan, and her husband, Tom. It becomes evident that their marriage is strained, partly due to Tom's extramarital affair with Myrtle Wilson.
  3. Gatsby's Parties:
    Gatsby, a mysterious millionaire with a shady past, hosts extravagant parties at his mansion. Despite the opulence, Gatsby seems elusive, and rumors circulate about his background.
  4. Gatsby's Love for Daisy:
    It is revealed that Gatsby and Daisy were in love before Daisy married Tom. Gatsby, now wealthy, bought his mansion in West Egg to be close to Daisy and throws the extravagant parties in the hope that she might attend one of them.
  5. Gatsby's Love for Daisy:

  6. Nick's Observations:
    Nick becomes a witness to the unfolding drama, observing the complexities of love, wealth, and societal expectations. He becomes the intermediary between Gatsby and Daisy, helping to arrange a meeting.
  7. Tragedy Unfolds:
    Gatsby and Daisy rekindle their romance, but the affair leads to tragic consequences. Tom discovers the affair and confronts Gatsby, ultimately leading to Gatsby's death.
  8. Nick's Reflections:
    The novel concludes with Nick reflecting on the American Dream, the illusion of wealth, and the moral decay of society. He becomes disillusioned with the superficiality and moral bankruptcy of those around him.
  9. Symbolism and Themes:
    "The Great Gatsby" explores themes of the American Dream, the corruption of wealth, and the illusion of social mobility. The green light at the end of Daisy's dock becomes a symbol of Gatsby's unattainable dreams.
  10. Narrative Style:
    Fitzgerald's narrative style is reflective and atmospheric, capturing the glamour and decadence of the Jazz Age while providing a critique of the underlying moral decay

    11. "The Great Gatsby" is considered a masterpiece of American literature, exploring the complexities of the American Dream and the disillusionment that follows the pursuit of wealth and social status. It remains a classic examination of the Roaring Twenties and the enduring quest for the elusive American Dream.
